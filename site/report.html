Recession Nowcasting, done in real time

Calibrated US & Canada recession probabilities from real-time vintages, benchmarked honestly against the term-spread probit and the Sahm rule.

1. The real-time problem

Recession-probability models are easy to make look good in hindsight and hard to trust in real time. The two traps are revisions (GDP, payrolls and the rest are heavily revised, so a backtest on today's data sees numbers nobody had at the time) and label lag (NBER and the C.D. Howe Council declare turning points 6–18 months late). This project treats both explicitly: every backtest decision at month τ uses only data released by τ, and the target is the eventual dating — the asymmetry that defines nowcasting.

2. Data & the vintage discipline

US monthly panel of — indicators (output, labour, housing, consumption, money/credit, rates, prices, sentiment), each put through its FRED-MD tcode stationarity transform. Labels: USREC (NBER) for the US, the C.D. Howe chronology for Canada. The VintageStore serves the panel as it was known at each τ; a unit test asserts no observation released after τ is ever used.

3. The benchmark ladder

Two transparent baselines that the machinery must beat to earn its keep:

  • Term-spread probit (Estrella–Mishkin): P(recession)=Φ(a+b·spread). Full-sample fit gives spread coefficient — (p=—), i.e. the canonical inverted-curve → recession signal.
  • Sahm rule: triggers when 3-month-average unemployment rises ≥0.5pp above its trailing-12-month low; trigger AUC —.

4. Primary models

The headline is a mixed-frequency dynamic factor model (statsmodels DynamicFactorMQ, Kalman filter/smoother with EM for missing data) feeding a probit. For the hundreds of expanding-window refits the backtest uses a fast PCA-EM factor approximation; the full state-space fit is reported separately and supplies the factor-state uncertainty. Two more comparators: a penalised (L1) MIDAS logistic on multi-lag indicators and a gradient-boosted nonlinear model on the factors.

5. Pseudo-real-time backtest

Expanding window, refit each month on vintage-available data, scored on Brier, log-loss, AUC, PR-AUC and calibration (ECE), with stationary-block-bootstrap 95% CIs and Diebold–Mariano / Giacomini–White predictive-ability tests against the benchmarks.

6. Uncertainty — a band, not a number

The deliverable is a probability band. We combine parameter uncertainty (block-bootstrap refit of the factor→recession probit) with factor-state uncertainty (Monte-Carlo draws from the smoother standard errors). Current US nowcast: — with a 90% band of —; mean band width —.

7. US vs Canada

The US leads (more episodes, a clean monthly USREC target). Canada is a second market with the C.D. Howe chronology and appropriately wider caveats — its turning points are sparser and annual-meeting-driven.

8. Robustness

  • COVID in/out: every model is scored on the full sample and excluding 2020-02..2020-08; the headline never rests on COVID. DFM Brier ex-COVID: —.
  • Vintage vs final: real-time AUC vs final-data AUC for the DFM — — vs —. Real-time skill ≤ final-data skill, as it must be; the gap is the timing penalty, not inflated skill.

9. Limitations

  • Few events. ≈9 US and ≈4–5 Canadian recessions since 1960 — intervals are wide by construction; no model is "validated" on so few episodes.
  • Regime change. The yield curve and other signals can shift; OOS skill is conditional on the future resembling the past.
  • Label lag & revision. Official dating arrives late and evolves.
  • COVID. A non-economic shock no macro model predicts; reported with and without, never "called".
  • Not investment advice. A recession probability is a research signal.
